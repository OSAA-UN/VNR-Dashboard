Electricity access and hydro installed capacity, 2015–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources

L'accès national à l'électricité est d'environ 20 % — très faible pour un pays disposant de 100 000 MW de potentiel hydroélectrique, the second largest in the world. The breakthrough: sector liberalisation since 2020 attracted private investment that grew installed capacity by 20%, with 3,282 MW under construction. By 2030, installed capacity is projected to double, which could transform access. Despite ongoing conflict in eastern regions, the review presents the energy-sector surge as its standout achievement.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.52 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 7.1.1
Basic water and sanitation access, 2015–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources
Basic water Basic sanitation

Basic drinking water access is improving — the RNV 2026 reports advances in the sector, with an institutional framework being finalised. Progress remains limited by the scale of need: DRC has over 100 million people, large areas inaccessible due to conflict, and a WASH gap where fewer than half the population has even basic service. Sanitation at ~22% is the deeper constraint, with open defecation rates still significant in rural areas.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.61–62 · WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme 2024 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 6.1.1
GDP growth, 2019–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources
Electricity access Installed capacity (indexed)

La RDC a maintenu une croissance du PIB supérieure à 5 % malgré la COVID-19 et le conflit à l'est and the unprecedented global ODA contraction — reaching 6.2% in 2023 and 5.9% in 2022. This resilience reflects the country's mining-led growth (cobalt, coltan, gold) and domestic-demand expansion. The review frames this as evidence that pro-welfare reforms have reduced inequalities despite interlocking crises.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.86–88, 120 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 17.1.1
External debt and fiscal position, 2015–2024

La RDC a géré sa position d'endettement grâce au point d'achèvement de l'IPPTE and subsequent concessional borrowing. The country faces a new challenge: the unprecedented global ODA contraction means traditional partners are withdrawing precisely as the PNSD investment programme requires financing. The review calls for strengthening partnerships under strain and new financing instruments.

Mise en œuvre conjointe des ODD et de l'Agenda 2063

La RDC aligne son Plan national stratégique de développement (PNSD) sur l'Agenda 2030 et l'Agenda 2063. All 17 SDGs are integrated into national planning; the 2026 VNR focuses on five goals (6, 7, 9, 11, 17) while reporting on progress across the full agenda. The review covers the 2020–2023 period and identifies SDG localisation as an area for strengthening. This is the DRC's third VNR submission (2020, 2023, 2026).

Durabilité de la dette et revenus

DRC's fiscal position reflects the tension between immense mineral wealth — cobalt, coltan, gold — and low public revenue collection. Sustained growth above 6% creates fiscal space, but the global ODA contraction means the country must accelerate domestic resource mobilisation as external support contracts.

External debt and fiscal position

DRC reached the HIPC completion point and has managed debt through concessional borrowing. The unprecedented global ODA crisis — partners withdrawing at scale — is the new challenge: debt sustainability requires replacing grant financing with private investment and domestic revenues, not more borrowing.

Sources : Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 17.4.1 · RNV 2026, p.121
Mining revenues and domestic resource mobilisation

Domestic revenues are growing, anchored by mineral royalties and a strengthening tax administration. GDP growth above 6% (2022–2023) generates fiscal space that the PNSD directs toward the five focus goals. The challenge is translating mining wealth into broad-based public revenue beyond the extractive enclave.

The 2026 review foregrounds water as a focus goal, with an institutional reform framework being finalised to extend the sector's reach across a country of 100 million spanning a territory larger than Western Europe.

The Congo Basin — abundance without delivery

Le fleuve Congo transporte environ 30 % du ruissellement d'eau douce africain — DRC is surrounded by water abundance. The challenge is entirely a governance and infrastructure problem: financing treatment plants, distribution networks and sanitation systems across a country larger than Western Europe. CICOS (International Commission of the Congo-Oubangui-Sangha Basin) provides the transboundary governance framework.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.72–73 · RNV 2020, p.85–86
Water and energy as linked investments

The Congo Basin's 3,000+ mm annual rainfall and river network make DRC water-abundant — the energy-water nexus runs the opposite direction to water-scarce peers. Hydropower (100,000 MW potential) uses the river for energy; the institutional challenge is using that same abundance for household water delivery. The 2026 review links both as priority investments.

Source : RNV 2026, p.61–62, 74
Water governance as the binding constraint

In a country where abundance of water, minerals and hydro energy coexists with ~40% basic water access and ~22% sanitation — the binding constraint is governance and infrastructure investment, not resources. The Année de la durabilité de l'eau for DRC is a year of institutional reform: finalising the sector framework, mobilising investment and extending delivery networks to conflict-affected and remote communities.

Source : RNV 2026, p.70–74
