National vs rural electrification, 2000–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources

National electrification reached 92.8% by 2024, driven by the Grand Poubara, Tchimbélé and Kinguélé hydroelectric plants — an 80-point rise over two decades. The gap with rural areas (26.9%) is structural: grid investment concentrated on cities. Hydropower expansion and rural mini-grids are the VNR's stated accelerators for closing the divide.

Urban water access has improved from ~55% to 60% since 2000, while rural access rose more slowly from ~20% to 30%. Greater Libreville faces a structural deficit of 50,000 m³/day despite abundant rainfall — the VNR frames this as a governance and infrastructure investment challenge, not a water scarcity problem.

Government revenue as a share of GDP, 2010–2024

Total government revenue Non-oil revenue

Gabon's revenue is highly oil-dependent: total revenues peaked above 35% of GDP during oil boom years and fell sharply when prices collapsed in 2014–2016. By 2024 the base stabilised around 21%, with non-oil revenues growing to ~13%. Reducing this volatility through diversification and non-oil tax reform is a stated PNDC priority.

Sources : RNV 2026 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 17.1.1
External debt stock, % of GDP, 2010–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources

External debt rose from ~29% of GDP in 2010 to a 43% peak in 2016 as the government borrowed to cover the oil-revenue shortfall. Infrastructure borrowing (ports, energy, roads) rebuilt the stock through 2020; it has since eased to ~36%. The CEMAC monetary union disciplines spending but also removes devaluation as an adjustment tool, making debt management the fiscal lever the PNDC must navigate.

Sources : Base de données ODD des ONU, Goal 17 · RNV 2026

Mise en œuvre conjointe des ODD et de l'Agenda 2063

Gabon anchors the 2030 Agenda and Agenda 2063 to Vision GABON-2050 and the Plan National de Développement et de la Croissance (PNDC). The PNDC translates the vision into sector plans, a multi-year public-investment programme and a monitoring framework, coordinated by a Steering Committee under the Presidency of the Transition Government. State, private sector and civil society participate through structured dialogue.

Durabilité de la dette et revenus

Gabon's fiscal position is shaped by oil: revenues fluctuate with prices, external debt was rebuilt after 2014 to cover the shortfall, and the PNDC's mission is diversifying the base before oil reserves diminish.

Debt service, external vs. internal

External debt rose from ~29% of GDP in 2010 to a 43% peak in 2016 as the government borrowed to cover the oil-revenue shortfall. It has since eased to ~36% — manageable but watched as the PNDC's investment programme continues within CEMAC monetary constraints.

Sources : Base de données ODD des ONU, Goal 17 · RNV 2026
Non-oil revenue, a decade of growth

Total government revenues fell sharply from the oil-boom peak (~38% of GDP in 2010) to ~21% after the 2014 oil price collapse. Non-oil revenues have grown from ~8% to ~13% of GDP over the decade — the structural diversification the PNDC is designed to accelerate further.

Sources : RNV 2026 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 17.1.1

In a water-rich equatorial country, Greater Libreville faces a 50,000 m³/day water shortage while rural areas have only 30% access. The VNR frames this as a governance and infrastructure challenge, not a resource scarcity problem — the rivers are full; the pipes are insufficient.

Libreville water infrastructure

Le Grand Libreville — qui abrite environ la moitié des 2,3 millions d'habitants du Gabon — est confronté à un déficit structurel de 50 000 m³/jour from urbanisation outpacing infrastructure investment. Aging pipes, distribution losses and insufficient treatment capacity combine to deliver less than demand. The PNDC includes new treatment plants, distribution rehabilitation and solar boreholes as the priority water investments.

Sources : RNV 2026
Forest as the water cycle

Gabon's 85% forest cover regulates the water cycle: forests intercept rainfall, recharge aquifers and maintain river baseflows. Forest conservation is therefore simultaneously the water-security strategy — the PNDC integrates REDD+ and carbon-market revenues into water-infrastructure investment, making the forest the financing vehicle for the rural water programme.

Source : RNV 2022 · RNV 2026
Water as governance, not resource

In one of Africa's most water-abundant countries, the challenge is not the resource but the infrastructure. Closing the 50,000 m³/day Libreville deficit and the rural water gap requires institutional reform, durable local infrastructure management and sustained investment — the Année de la durabilité de l'eau is therefore a year of governance and investment for Gabon.

Source : RNV 2026
