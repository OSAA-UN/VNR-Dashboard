Electricity access, national vs rural, 2020–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources

L'accès national à l'électricité est passé de 33 % en 2020 à 43,7 % en 2024, porté par le programme d'intégration hydroélectrique régionale de l'OMVG that connects Guinea-Bissau to cross-border clean hydro from shared Gambia River basin projects. The urban-rural gap — 65.6% vs 24.9% — and near-zero clean-cooking access (2%) mark the household energy challenge that regional grid integration has not yet reached.

Water and sanitation access, 2022–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources
Basic water Basic sanitation Safely managed water

Basic drinking water access sits at 52.6% and has been effectively flat — a structural challenge the PND 2026–2035 addresses directly. Safely-managed water reaches only 24% of the population, and basic sanitation only 28.9%. The VNR frames universal basic WASH as the foundation condition for human capital progress, recognising that health and educational outcomes cannot improve without it.

Sources : RNV 2026 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicators 6.1.1, 6.2.1, 1.4.1
GDP growth and inflation, 2022–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources
GDP growth Inflation

La stabilisation macroéconomique mise en avant par le RNV 2026 : la croissance du PIB s'est maintenue entre 4 et 6 % across three years, and inflation was tamed from 9.4% in 2022 to 3.8% in 2024 within the WAEMU/BCEAO monetary framework. This stability is the economic base the PND 2026–2035 builds on — though government revenue at just 8.6% of GDP remains among the lowest in the world, keeping the state heavily ODA-dependent.

Sources : Base de données ODD des ONU, Goal 17 series · RNV 2026
External debt stock, % of GNI, 2019–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources

External debt has hovered near 67–68% of GNI since 2019 — a high and stable position that reflects structural ODA and concessional-loan dependence rather than a debt crisis. Combined with tax revenues of only 8.6% of GDP, the fiscal space for SDG investment is narrow. Macroeconomic stabilisation — growth at 4–6% and inflation tamed to 3.8% — is the 2026 VNR's answer to the question of how the PND will be financed.

Sources : Base de données ODD des ONU, Goal 17 series · RNV 2026

Mise en œuvre conjointe des ODD et de l'Agenda 2063

Guinea-Bissau does not run a separate tracking tool for the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals and the African Union's Agenda 2063. Instead, both agendas are built directly into the country's own planning documents, the National Development Plan 2018–2027, the long-term Vision Guinea-Bissau 2040–2060, and a "4P" monitoring framework that groups all 17 goals under four pillars: Population, Prosperity, Planet, and Peace & Partnerships.

Durabilité de la dette et revenus

Two sides of the same public-finance story: what Guinea-Bissau spends servicing its debt, and what it raises at home to fund development.

External debt and macro stabilisation

External debt has hovered near 67–68% of GNI since 2019, a stable but constraining position. Within the WAEMU/BCEAO monetary framework, fiscal reform and domestic revenue mobilisation are the only levers available to improve the SDG financing position.

Sources : Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 17.4.1 · RNV 2026, 121
GDP growth and inflation, 2022–2024

GDP growth held at 4–6% across three years while inflation was tamed from 9.4% (2022) to 3.8% (2024) — the macrostabilisation the VNR presents as the economic foundation for PND delivery. Tax revenue at 8.6% of GDP remains the fiscal gap the plan must close.

Sources : RNV 2026, 86–88, 120 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 17.1.1

Guinea-Bissau is among West Africa's most water-abundant countries — the Geba, Cacheu and Corubal river systems provide plentiful freshwater and water stress is near zero. The challenge is not the resource but access and quality: only 24% have safely-managed water and 22.6% safely-managed sanitation.

IWRM and transboundary water

Fishing on Lake Tanganyika is regulated under the Lake Tanganyika Authority, a body shared with neighbouring countries; Guinea-Bissau's membership of the Nile Basin Initiative provides a second standing regional framework.

Sources : RNV 2026, 72–73 · RNV 2022, p.85–86
Coastal and island vulnerability

Restoration of the Nyamabuno marsh and Ntahangwa river banks, buffer zones around Lakes Tanganyika and Cohoha, rehabilitation of the Buterere wastewater station, and the national reforestation drive "Ewe Guinea-Bissau Urambaye", the resilience layer that makes durable water access possible.

Source : RNV 2026, 61–62, 74
Water as a human capital condition

With safely-managed water at 24% and sanitation at 22.6%, waterborne disease is a leading cause of child morbidity and school absenteeism. The PND 2026–2035 treats universal basic WASH as the health and education foundation without which its human capital investment cannot yield returns. Water sustainability for Guinea-Bissau is a development-strategy question, not only a resource-management one.

Source : RNV 2026, 70–74
