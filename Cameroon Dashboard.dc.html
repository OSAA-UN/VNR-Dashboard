Electricity access, national vs rural, 2000–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources

National electricity access climbed from 41% in 2000 to 72% in 2024, a 31-point gain driven by hydro-led investment (the renewable mix is ~80%). The rural-urban divide remains critical: cities are at ~96% while rural areas reached only 42.7%. The SND30's rural electrification programme targets this gap as the key SDG 7 priority for the decade.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.52 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 7.1.1
Basic water and sanitation access, 2000–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources
Basic water Basic sanitation

Basic drinking-water access rose from 55.8% in 2000 to 71.4% in 2024, a 15.6-point gain. Sanitation is the harder story: only 47.1% have basic service, 37.8% basic handwashing, and 4.4% still practise open defecation. The RNV 2026 flags sanitation as the structural challenge requiring greater investment.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.61–62 · WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme 2024 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 6.1.1

Account ownership among adults nearly quintupled in a decade, from 12.9% in 2011 to about 61% by 2024, driven almost entirely by mobile money. This is Cameroon's clearest development win — though a gender gap persists (53% for women vs 69% for men) and social-protection coverage remains thin at ~10% of the population.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.86–88, 120 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 17.1.1

External debt fell from 105.9% of GNI in 2000 to 10.2% after the 2006 HIPC completion point, then rebuilt to 36.9% by 2020 as infrastructure investment was financed through borrowing. It has since eased to 31.3% — a manageable but watched level as the SND30's investment programme continues.

Mise en œuvre conjointe des ODD et de l'Agenda 2063

Cameroon does not run a separate tracking tool for the SDGs and Agenda 2063. Both are integrated into the SND30 (National Development Strategy 2020–2030), the operational decade of Vision 2035. The SND30 organises public policy around structural transformation, human capital, employment and governance, with participatory, indicator-based monitoring coordinated by the Ministry of Economy.

SND30 & Vision 2035 — the single shared anchor

The SND30 serves as the single reference frame for both the SDGs and Agenda 2063, with all 17 goals integrated into sector ministry plans and the national budget. Decentralisation runs through 10 regional councils (since 2020) and 360+ communes, with the North-West and South-West under special-status devolution.

Financement the Goals

Cameroon's financing base is narrow: government revenue of ~14% of GDP, foreign investment under 2% and official flows in the hundreds of millions. The review's strategy leans on domestic revenue mobilisation, concessional finance for infrastructure and the local financial deepening that mobile money has begun.

Durabilité de la dette et revenus

Two sides of the same macroeconomic story: the debt burden rebuilt by infrastructure borrowing after HIPC relief, and the imported-inflation shock of 2022–2023 that CEMAC monetary discipline is now unwinding.

External debt stocks, % of GNI

External debt fell from >105% of GNI to 10.2% after the 2006 HIPC completion, then was rebuilt to 36.9% as infrastructure was financed. It has since eased to 31.3% — a manageable and watched level as the SND30's investment programme continues.

Sources : Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 17.4.1 · RNV 2026, p.121
Inflation tamed — 2022–2025

Two decades of CFA-franc discipline kept inflation mostly under 3% until imported food and fuel costs pushed it to 7.4% in 2023 (Base de données ODD des ONU). It eased to 4.5% by 2024 and, per the review, 3.4% by 2025 — closing back toward the CEMAC convergence ceiling.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.86–88, 120 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 17.1.1

Année de la durabilité de l'eau

In a country with among the lowest water-withdrawal rates on the continent (~1.6% of renewable supply), the challenge is infrastructure and governance. Basic water access rose from 55.8% to 71.4% since 2000 — but sanitation (47.1%) and handwashing (37.8%) remain the structural gaps.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.61–63, 73 · Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 6.1.1
Water abundance without delivery

Cameroon withdraws only ~1.6% of its renewable freshwater supply — abundant but not delivered. The transboundary governance challenge is Lake Chad: Cameroon's Far North region borders a shrinking lake that drives displacement, food insecurity and conflict. The Lake Chad Basin Commission and Niger Basin Authority provide the regional frameworks.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.72–73 · RNV 2020, p.85–86
Water and energy — the hydro connection

Cameroon's rivers do double duty: hydropower keeps the energy mix ~80% renewable and new dams anchor the SND30's industrialisation plans. The review calls this renewable base a major transition asset — provided transmission reaches the 57% of rural residents still without power.

Source : RNV 2026, p.61–62, 74
Sanitation as the binding constraint

In a country withdrawing less than 2% of its renewable water supply, the constraint is not the resource but infrastructure and investment. The VNR's message: the Année de la durabilité de l'eau is a year of sanitation investment for Cameroon — building treatment, sewerage and hygiene infrastructure alongside the water-supply gains already achieved.

Source : RNV 2026, p.70–74
