GDP growth recovered from 2.4% (FY2023/24) to 4.4% in FY2024/25, driven by private-sector-led and diversified growth across digital, green energy, tourism and manufacturing — the reform dividend the review presents as Egypt's fourth-VNR headline. Social protection & Hayah Kareema coverage, 2015–2024
Hover over points for values and sources
Hayah Kareema / social protection Financial inclusion

Hayah Kareema ("Decent Life") has reached 58 million Egyptians across 1,500 villages since 2019 — one of the largest rural development programmes on the continent. Takaful & Karama, the conditional and unconditional cash-transfer system, has expanded social protection from near-zero to coverage of millions of the most vulnerable households, while financial inclusion instruments (Meeza cards, women's bank accounts) extend the reach into informal economies.

Sources: VNR 2026, p.61–62 · WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme 2024 · UN SDG Database, indicator 6.1.1

Joint Implementation of the SDGs & Agenda 2063

Egypt presents its fourth VNR, having submitted in 2016, 2018, 2021 and 2026 — the most review cycles of any country in this cohort. The 2030 Agenda is embedded in Vision Egypt 2030, with the Integrated National Financing Framework (INFF) aligning financing flows with SDG investments. Voluntary Local Reviews (VLRs) extend SDG localization to the municipal level, with Egypt leading Africa in this practice.

Debt Sustainability & Revenue

Egypt's fiscal trajectory reflects the reform programme: external debt rose through COVID-19 support spending and was then managed through the reform-led growth acceleration (4.4% in FY2024/25). The Ras El-Hekma deal and FDI inflows provide the private financing that reduces government debt dependency.

The Nile and water sovereignty

The Nile provides 97% of Egypt's freshwater. Transboundary governance — including the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) — is a defining water-security challenge. Egypt invests in water efficiency (irrigation modernisation targeting 20% reduction in agricultural water use), desalination and treated-water reuse to reduce per-capita freshwater demand.

NWFE — water-food-energy nexus

The NWFE Programme (Nexus of Water-Food-Energy) is Egypt's integrated response to water scarcity: renewable energy powers desalination and drip irrigation; treated wastewater is reused for agriculture; solar-powered pumps modernise the irrigation network. These instruments reduce freshwater pressure while simultaneously advancing SDG 2 (food security) and SDG 7 (clean energy).

Source: VNR 2026, p.61–62, 74
Water scarcity as Egypt's defining SDG constraint

At 112.9% water stress, Egypt has the most acute water challenge of any country in this cohort. The Year of Water Sustainability is a year of water-efficiency and desalination investment for Egypt — every drop saved through irrigation modernisation, treated-water reuse and reduced losses extends the country's development horizon. The NWFE nexus approach links water directly to energy and food security as inseparable goals.

Source: VNR 2026, p.70–74
