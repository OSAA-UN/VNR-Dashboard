GDP growth Pre-crisis average

Croissance fell to 1.5% in 2022 as security disruptions hit agriculture, mining and supply chains. By 2025 it recovered to 5.3% — marginally above the 2016–2022 average of 5.2% — through reforms and a productive revival driven by the "Produire local" strategy. The review presents this as a sovereignty dividend: local production reducing external vulnerabilities.

Burkina Faso operates within the WAEMU/BCEAO monetary framework (CFA franc), providing price discipline but limiting monetary tools. Extraordinary security expenditure compresses fiscal space: military operations, IDP support and emergency food relief reduce the investment available for water, energy and social services. Gold and cotton revenues are the primary domestic anchors, with the UEMOA regional bond market for sovereign borrowing.

Durabilité de la dette et revenus

Burkina Faso operates within the WAEMU/BCEAO monetary union (CFA franc), delivering structural price stability but removing monetary-adjustment tools. Security expenditure compresses fiscal space; growth recovering to 5.3% is the primary debt-sustainability argument the review makes.

Croissance recovery — from crisis to resilience

Croissance returned to 5.3% — above the long-run pre-crisis average — through reforms and the "Produire local" productive revival. This is presented as evidence that the sovereign economic strategy is delivering despite the security context, and as the fiscal foundation for sustaining water, energy and social services.

Sources : Base de données ODD des ONU, indicator 17.4.1 · RNV 2026, p.121
UEMOA monetary framework and price discipline

Within the UEMOA/BCEAO framework, Burkina Faso has CFA franc price stability but no independent monetary tools. The review's growth recovery relies entirely on supply-side reforms — "Produire local," energy sovereignty, and the reconquest that allows economic activity to resume in previously disrupted areas.

The Sahelian water challenge

Burkina Faso faces genuine water scarcity in the Sahelian north: irregular rainfall, groundwater as the primary source, and climate change extending droughts while reducing recharge. The 21.1% still without safe water live predominantly in the same areas most affected by displacement — reaching them requires solar-powered borehole deepening and conflict-sensitive infrastructure.

Sources : RNV 2026, p.72–73 · RNV 2020, p.85–86
Water-energy nexus

Solar mini-grids and solar borehole systems link the energy and water transitions in one rural infrastructure investment. The sovereign energy strategy targets 50%+ renewables by 2030 — solar parks reduce diesel dependence while mini-grids power the pumps that extend water access to off-grid communities and IDP sites.

Source : RNV 2026, p.61–62, 74
Water in a conflict context

The 1.99M IDPs face acute water insecurity: camps and host communities rely on trucked water and emergency boreholes. Climate pressure compounds insecurity: irregular rainfall reduces agricultural productivity in areas already under stress. The Année de la durabilité de l'eau for Burkina Faso is a year of conflict-sensitive water delivery — a governance and logistics challenge as much as a finance and infrastructure one.

Source : RNV 2026, p.70–74
