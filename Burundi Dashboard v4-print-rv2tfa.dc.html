Accès à l'électricité, 2017–2025

Electricity access has nearly tripled since 2017 — from 9.3% to 25.9% of the population — driven by new hydropower and solar capacity. The United Nations SDG Database records a slightly lower 20.1% for 2024 (marked in amber), a gap between national and official figures worth noting when comparing across countries.

Improved water access — urban vs. rural, 2017–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources
Urbain Rural National

À l'échelle nationale, l'accès à l'eau potable améliorée est passé de 82,3 % à 86,3 % entre 2017 et 2024. Les progrès se concentrent dans les villes : la couverture urbaine est quasi universelle à 97,4 %, tandis que la couverture rurale est retombée de 85,6 % en 2020 à 84,4 % — making rural water supply the clearest opportunity for further progress.

Recettes publiques en % de l'économie, 2015–2024
Survolez les points pour les valeurs et sources
Total government revenue Internally-raised revenue

Government revenue has grown steadily — from 13.4% of gross domestic product (GDP) in 2015 to 17.7% in 2024 — supported by digitalized tax collection. Tax pressure remains at 13.1%, below the benchmark set out in the 2025 Sevilla Commitment on financing for development, so further domestic revenue growth is a stated national priority.

Sources: VNR 2026, p.86–88, 120 · UN SDG Database, indicator 17.1.1
Debt service — external vs. internal, 2017–2024

Since 2023, servicing debt owed to foreign lenders has cost Burundi more than servicing domestic debt — reaching 10.9% of export earnings in 2023, per the United Nations SDG Database. The review presents this shift as grounds for the "urgency of ongoing reforms" and for seeking financing on more favourable (concessional) terms. Mise en œuvre conjointe des ODD & de l'Agenda 2063

Le Burundi ne dispose pas d'un outil de suivi distinct pour les Objectifs de développement durable des Nations Unies et l'Agenda 2063 de l'Union africaine. Instead, both agendas are built directly into the country's own planning documents — the National Development Plan 2018–2027, the long-term Vision Burundi 2040–2060, and a "4P" monitoring framework that groups all 17 goals under four pillars: Population, Prosperity, Planet, and Peace & Partnerships.

National Development Plan 2018–2027 — the shared anchor

Un plan national unique sert conjointement les Objectifs de développement durable, l'Agenda 2063 et la Vision Burundi 2040–2060. Delivery runs through dedicated finance institutions — the Impulse, Guarantee and Support Fund (FIGA), the Youth Investment Bank (BIJE) and the Women's Investment and Development Bank (BIDF) — and budgeting organized around programmes rather than ministries.

Debt Sustainability & Domestic Resource Mobilization

Deux aspects de la même histoire des finances publiques : ce que le Burundi dépense pour le service de sa dette et ce qu'il lève au niveau national pour financer le développement.

Debt service — external vs. internal

Servicing debt owed to foreign lenders cost 10.9% of export earnings in 2023 — overtaking domestic debt service that year. The review describes a "high debt level" and "limited debt-management capacity" and recommends financing on more favourable (concessional) terms.

Sources: UN SDG Database, indicator 17.4.1 · VNR 2026, p.121
Domestic revenue — a decade of growth

Les recettes publiques totales sont passées de 13,4 % à 17,7 % du produit intérieur brut entre 2015 et 2024, and internally-raised revenue from 7.8% to 10.6% — supported by a digital taxpayer identification number and digitalized tax and customs collection. Property-tax and anti-fraud reforms are the next steps the review recommends.

Sources: VNR 2026, p.86–88, 120 · UN SDG Database, indicator 17.1.1

2026 Année de la gestion durable de l'eau

The African Union's theme for 2026. In Burundi, water access improved from 82.3% to 86.3% between 2017 and 2024, while Lake Tanganyika is at once the country's richest resource and its most disruptive flood risk.

Gouvernance transfrontalière de l'eau

Fishing on Lake Tanganyika is regulated under the Lake Tanganyika Authority, a body shared with neighbouring countries; Burundi's membership of the Nile Basin Initiative provides a second standing regional framework.

Sources: VNR 2026, p.72–73 · VNR 2020, p.85–86

Water security as human security

Lake overflows link directly to displacement, health problems, school dropout and water pollution — the 2020–2024 flood cycle destroyed housing along the lakeshore and in Gatumba. The review treats reliable water management as a foundation of social stability.

Source: VNR 2026, p.70–74
Restauration des écosystèmes hydriques

Restoration of the Nyamabuno marsh and Ntahangwa river banks, buffer zones around Lakes Tanganyika and Cohoha, rehabilitation of the Buterere wastewater station, and the national reforestation drive "Ewe Burundi Urambaye" — the resilience layer that makes durable water access possible.

Source: VNR 2026, p.61–62, 74
